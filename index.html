Spotify Song Analysis

Overall Introduction

The music industry has always played a big role in culture and entertainment, but as time goes on, the way that people discover and consume music has changed a lot. Streaming platforms shape listening habits across the world, and Spotify stands out as one of the biggest and most influential services with over 100 million of songs with many different genre and audiences. Considering this, an important question emerges: what makes certain songs more popular that others? And more specifically, do popular songs share similar audio characteristics, and do those characteristic vary by genre?

This project explores the relationship between a song's measurable features and its popularity within its genre using Spotify's track dataset. Songs can be described not only by their title, artist, or genre, but also by quantitative attributes such as danceability, energy, loudness, tempo, duration, valence, and acousticness. These features provide a way to analyze music beyond personal opinion and taste. By comparing these characteristics across genres, we aim to identify whether highly popular songs tend to follow certain patterns and whether those patterns differ between genres.

The goal of this analysis is not simply to determine which songs are popular, but to better understand why they become popular. For example, one genre may favor high-energy and danceable tracks, while another may show stronger popularity among songs with lower tempo or more acoustic qualities. Studying these relationships can reveal how musical structure connects with listener preferences and how genres develop their own successful formulas over time.

We believe that this topic is especially valuable because it connects data science with modern digital media and cultural trends. Music popularity is often discussed in subjective terms, but datasets like Spotify's allow us to examine popularity from a data-driven perspective. And the insights from this project can help explain industry trends, audience behavior, and the characteristics that may contribute to viral or commercially successful songs. More broadly, this research shows how quantitative analysis can be used to better understand creative industries, turning musical features into meaningful patterns that reflect what listeners respond to most.

Introduction to the data

Our analysis makes use of a dataset called Spotify_Song_Attributes_Cleaned.csv, which contains audio features and metadata for songs collected from Spotify. The dataset includes information about track characteristics such as danceability, energy, valence, speechiness, acousticness, instrumentalness, tempo, loudness, popularity, and genre labels. These variables allow us to examine how songs differ across genres and how audio features relate to one another. The detail information about the features are listed in the following table:

Major Genre Grouped genre category used in the analysis

Meanwhile, we also believe that the underlying audio features provided by Spotify are essential for understanding patterns in modern music. Therefore, we make use of Spotify's song attribute dataset, which contains detailed numerical features such as danceability, energy, valence, tempo, and loudness. These attributes allow us to analyze songs quantitatively and uncover relationships between musical characteristics across different tracks and genres.

The Relationship Between Spotify Audio Features

Before diving into genre comparisons, we first examine the relationships between different Spotify audio features to understand which features are most strongly related and to guide the feature choices used in the rest of our analysis. Since all selected features are numerical, we create a correlation heatmap to explore whether there are linear relationships between variables such as danceability, energy, valence, loudness, and others. A higher absolute correlation value indicates a stronger linear relationship, while values close to zero suggest little to no linear association between two features.

Based on the heatmap, some audio features show strong relationships. For example, energy has a strong positive correlation with loudness, indicating that more energetic songs tend to be louder. In contrast, acousticness has a strong negative correlation with energy (-0.77), suggesting that acoustic tracks are generally less intense. Danceability and valence also show moderate positive relationships, meaning more danceable songs often sound more positive or upbeat. Meanwhile, features like tempo and speechiness tend to have weaker correlations with others, indicating they capture more independent aspects of a song. These findings directly inform our subsequent visualizations, where we focus on features like valence, acousticness, and energy that show the most meaningful variation across genres.

Valence by Genre

Building on the heatmap, we use valence to show how musical positivity differs across genres. Using a box plot in this case is effective because it clearly compares each genre's median, spread, and outliers in one view, while sorting genres by median valence makes the pattern easier to read and interpret. The interactive features within this figure allow users to hover for details, zoom in on specific genres, pan across the figure, and inspect outliers more closely. From this graph we can see that positivity is not distributed evenly across genres, as some tend to have higher valence, some lower, and some that have a greater distribution of mood than others, showing that genre influences musical mood in different ways.

Average Audio Features Across Genres

We continue to explore the feel by adding more variables into the equation to help us see the overall vibe of music within a certain genre and how they compare to others. Using a grouped bar chart we show the average values of danceability, energy, valence, and acousticness across genres. With the different combinations of these variables, we can tell how a song would sound or make people feel. For example, having high danceability, high energy, and high valence translates into a party song, while songs with low energy, high acousticness, and mid/low valence translate into a song that is intimate, mellow, and reflective. The interactive feature in this graph allows users to hover over the bars and see the exact values for each feature. From this visualization, we learned that genres differ across multiple audio traits, and that the feature linked to popularity is likely dependent on genre rather than following a single formula for all songs.

This scatter plot explores the relationship between acousticness and energy across different music genres on Spotify. Each point represents a song, and the dropdown menu allows you to isolate a single genre to see how it clusters in the acousticness-energy space. This pairing was chosen because acousticness and energy have the strongest negative correlation (-0.77) in the dataset, meaning songs that are more acoustic tend to be significantly less intense. Notably, Classical and Lo-Fi/Chill songs cluster in the high acousticness, low energy region, while EDM and Rock/Metal songs sit in the opposite corner with low acousticness and high energy. Pop and Hip-Hop/Rap songs are more spread across the middle, suggesting greater variety within those genres. Overall, the visualization confirms that acousticness and energy together are strong indicators of a song's sonic character and genre identity.

Distribution of Song Duration by Genre

This set of histograms displays the distribution of song duration (in minutes) across each major genre on Spotify. Classical and Soundtrack/Score genres tend to have the widest spread in song length, with many tracks exceeding 5 minutes, reflecting the nature of orchestral and film compositions. In contrast, Pop, Hip-Hop/Rap, and EDM songs are tightly clustered between 2 and 4 minutes, aligning with the standard radio-friendly song format. Lo-Fi/Chill songs also show a narrow distribution, suggesting that the genre follows consistent length conventions. Overall, song duration appears to be a genre-defining characteristic, with certain genres showing much more variation than others.

Summary and Additional Work

Through this analysis, we explored how audio features vary across genres on Spotify and what patterns emerge from the data. From the valence boxplot, we found that musical positivity is not evenly distributed across genres. Soundtrack/Score and Classical songs tend to skew lower in valence, while Country and Bollywood/Indian songs are generally more upbeat. The correlation heatmap revealed that energy and loudness are strongly positively correlated, while acousticness and energy have a strong negative relationship, meaning that more acoustic songs tend to be less intense. The grouped bar chart confirmed that genres differ significantly across multiple audio traits simultaneously. Classical songs stand out with very high acousticness and low danceability, while EDM and Pop cluster around high energy and danceability. The scatter plot showed that while genre does influence where songs fall in the acousticness-energy space, there is still considerable overlap between genres like Pop, Hip-Hop/Rap, and R&B/Soul, suggesting these genres share similar sonic qualities. Finally, the duration histograms revealed that song length is also genre-dependent, with Classical and Soundtrack/Score tracks spanning a much wider range while Pop and Lo-Fi/Chill songs follow a tight, predictable format. Overall, this analysis shows that genre is a strong predictor of a song's audio characteristics, and that different genres follow their own internal rules when it comes to sound.

For future work, incorporating an explicit popularity score would allow us to directly test which audio features drive streaming success within each genre, rather than relying on play time as a proxy. Adding a time dimension to track how genre characteristics have shifted over decades would also provide valuable insight into how listener preferences have evolved.
