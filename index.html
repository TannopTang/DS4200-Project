Spotify song analysis

Overall Introduction

The music industry has always played a big role in culture and entertainment, but as time goes on, the way that people discover and consume music has changed a lot. Streaming platforms shape listening habits across the world, and Spotify stands out as one of the biggest and most influential services with over 100 million of songs with many different genre and audiences. Considering this an import question emerges: what makes certain songs more popular that others? And more specifically, do popular songs share similar audio characteristics, and do those characteristic vary by genre?

This project explores the relationship between a song’s measurable features and its popularity within its genre using Spotify’s track dataset. Songs can be described not only by their title, artist, or genre, but also by quantitative attributes such as danceability, energy, loudness, tempo, duration, valence, and acousticness. These features provide a way to analyze music beyond personal opinion and taste. By comparing these characteristics across genres, we aim to identify whether highly popular songs tend to follow certain patterns and whether those patterns differ between genres.

The goal of this analysis is not simply to determine which songs are popular, but to better understand why they become popular. For example, one genre may favor high-energy and danceable tracks, while another may show stronger popularity among songs with lower tempo or more acoustic qualities. Studying these relationships can reveal how musical structure connects with listener preferences and how genres develop their own successful formulas over time.

We believe that this topic is especially valuable because it connects data science with modern digital media and cultural trends. Music popularity is often discussed in subjective terms, but datasets like Spotify’s allow us to examine popularity from a data-driven perspective. And the insights from this project can help explain industry trends, audience behavior, and the characteristics that may contribute to viral or commercially successful songs. More broadly, this research shows how quantitative analysis can be used to better understand creative industries, turning musical features into meaningful patterns that reflect what listeners respond to most.

Introduction to the data

Our analysis makes use of two datasets. the first data is a data set called `us_statewide_crime.csv` which contains the following crime statistics for all US states and the District of Columbia in 2010. The data has 50 rows with 9 features. The detail information about the features are listed in the following table:

Variable Name Details
State State name
Violent_Crime Violent crime rate (per 100,000 residents)
Murder_Rate Murder rate (per 100,000 residents)
Poverty Percent of residents below the poverty rate
High_School Percent of residents with a high school degree
College Percent of residents with a four-year college degree
Single_Parent Percent of children living in single parent families
Unemployed Unemployment rate
Metropolitan Percent of residents living in a metropolitan area

Meanwhile, we also believe that the geographical information is important when evaluating the unemployment rate. Therefore, we also make use of geographical information on Natural Earth. The website provides the geographical information for making maps for US states.

Valence by Genre

This box plot shows how valence, or the musical positivity of a song, differs across Spotify genres. Valence measures how happy, cheerful, or positive a track sounds. By comparing the distributions for each genre, we can see which genres tend to have more upbeat songs and which tend to have lower or more varied valence values.

In general, west coast states tend to have higher unemployment rate. On the east coast, West virginia and New York state tend to have higher unemplyment rate than the others. In the south, Mississippi and Louisiana are the ones with the much higher unemplyment rate.

Overview of the Unemployment rate

First, we present an overview of the unemployment rate for all 50 states. Alaska is the one with the highest unemployment rate, which may due to the seasonal business in the state. The effectiveness of labor market policies and governance structures can influence unemployment rates. Washington, D.C., being the seat of government, may experience fluctuations in employment related to government spending and policies. Mississippi and Louisiana have economies that are heavily reliant on sectors such as agriculture, manufacturing, and energy. Economic downturns or fluctuations in these industries can impact employment opportunities. Connecticut, South Dakota and Virginia have the lowest unemplyment rate. These states often have diverse economies with strong sectors such as technology, healthcare, finance, and tourism. A diverse economy can provide more resilience to economic downturns.

The relationship between unemployment rate and other social factors

Moreover, we also want to examine the relationship between unemployment rate and other social factors in the data. All features are numerical thus we firts create a correlation plot to examine whether there are linear relationship between unemployment rate with other social factors. Notice that if the correlation is small, it only means that there is no potential linear relationship between two features.

Based on the correlation plot, among all the features, poverty has the strongest correlation with the unemployment rate since they are both related to the economics. The percentage of single parents is the second important feature which inidicates the stability of family is also strongly related to the job market. The increase of unemployment rate may also related to the increase of violent crime and murder rate but has a negative relationship with education indicators, like the percentage of people who go to high school and college. The only variable that has no direct linear relation with the unemployment rate is Metropolitan, which means the percent of residents living in a metropolitan area.

This scatter plot below explores the relationship between danceability and energy across different music genres on Spotify. Each point represents a song, colored by its major genre. Using the dropdown menu, you can isolate a single genre to see how it clusters in the danceability-energy space. Notably, EDM songs tend to cluster in the high energy, moderate-to-high danceability region, while Classical music sits in the low energy, low danceability corner. Pop and Hip-Hop/Rap songs are more spread out, suggesting greater variety within those genres. Overall, the visualization reveals that genre is a strong indicator of where a song falls on both the danceability and energy spectrum.

Distribution of Song Duration by Genre

This set of histograms displays the distribution of song duration (in minutes) across each major genre on Spotify. Classical and Soundtrack/Score genres tend to have the widest spread in song length, with many tracks exceeding 5 minutes, reflecting the nature of orchestral and film compositions. In contrast, Pop, Hip-Hop/Rap, and EDM songs are tightly clustered between 2 and 4 minutes, aligning with the standard radio-friendly song format. Lo-Fi/Chill songs also show a narrow distribution, suggesting that the genre follows consistent length conventions. Overall, song duration appears to be a genre-defining characteristic, with certain genres showing much more variation than others.
